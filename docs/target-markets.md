# Target Markets — Ranked by Pain Level and Product Fit

## 1. Offshore Oil and Gas Platforms

**Pain level: Extreme**

- 35% of unexpected shutdowns caused by avoidable equipment failures (IOGP data)
- Downtime costs: $500K/hour
- Unplanned downtime surged 76%, reaching $149M per site
- Maintenance = 30-40% of annual operating budget
- Helicopter technician visits for FTIR repair: $10K-50K per trip
- Constant vibration, salt spray, humidity, temperature swings

**Current detection:** NDIR point sensors + periodic FTIR surveys. FTIR systems need climate-controlled enclosures on platforms.

**SFG advantage:** Solid-state, sealed housing, no moving parts, no technician visits for mirror alignment. Multi-gas from one unit replaces multiple NDIR sensors.

**Key operators:** Shell, BP, Equinor (Gulf of Mexico and North Sea)

**Regulations:** OSHA confined space gas detection (O2, combustibles, H2S, CO). BSEE oversight.

## 2. Military CBRN Detection

**Pain level: High**

- JCAD Increment 1 cannot detect most toxic industrial chemicals
- LCD 3.3 replacement: improved sensitivity but still single-technology (IMS)
- Total JCAD procurement: $350M+
- CBRN defense market: $17.93B (2023), projected $29.96B by 2032
- Chemical/Biological Defense Program budget FY2025: $1.66B
- CBRN Dismounted Recon procurement: $74.1M

**Current detection:** Ion Mobility Spectrometry (JCAD/LCD 3.3). Consumable parts, calibration needed, limited chemical library.

**SFG advantage:** No consumables, no calibration drift. Physics-based selectivity doesn't degrade. Ruggedized for field use. Multi-agent detection from one device.

**What military wants (from procurement docs):** Better sensitivity, fewer false alarms, longer battery life, lower lifecycle cost, toxic industrial chemical detection.

**Procurement path:** SBIR/STTR grants, JPEO-CBRND (Joint Program Executive Office), DTRA (Defense Threat Reduction Agency).

## 3. HazMat / First Responders

**Pain level: High**

- NIJ finding: NO commercially available gas detector meets ALL first responder criteria
- Size vs capability tradeoff — wearable detectors limited to 1-5 gases, multi-gas too bulky
- Many emergency vehicles carry NO chemical detection equipment
- Colorimetric tubes (primary tool) hard to read in poor light, noisy, smoky environments
- Training gaps on equipment use

**Current detection:** MSA HAZMATCAD (SAW sensors), handheld Raman, handheld FTIR (TruDefender ~$7,590). FTIR breaks on impact.

**SFG advantage:** Solid-state survives drops and impacts. Multi-chemical ID without spectral interpretation. "Which channel lit up" is binary — minimal training needed.

**Regulations:** NFPA 470 (HazMat response), NFPA 472 (responder competence).

**Customers:** Fire departments, HazMat teams, emergency management agencies. Equipment purchased through DHS grants, FEMA Preparedness Grants.

## 4. Mining (Underground)

**Pain level: Critical (lives at stake)**

- Methane ignitions: ~379 reported incidents (2002-2012) with continuous mining machines
- Paradise #9 Mine (2019): fatality from methane explosion when detector alarmed but operator continued work
- MSHA requires: methane warning at 1.0-1.5%, shutoff at 2.0%. CO alert at 5 ppm, alarm at 10 ppm.
- 500 consecutive successful tests required for MSHA certification
- Vibration from blasting, dust, water intrusion destroy sensors

**Current detection:** Catalytic bead sensors (methane), electrochemical cells (CO). Drift, poisoning, short lifespan.

**SFG advantage:** No chemical degradation (PPLN is inert crystal). Doesn't get poisoned by silicone or H2S like catalytic sensors. Multi-gas (CH4 + CO + O2 deficiency) from one unit.

**Regulations:** MSHA 30 CFR Part 27 (methane detection), Part 75 (mandatory safety standards).

## 5. Nuclear Facilities (Seismic Zones)

**Pain level: Moderate-High (regulatory driven)**

- 104 US nuclear facilities, many near seismic zones
- IEEE 344-2013: seismic qualification standard for nuclear equipment
- Post-Fukushima: FLEX mitigation strategy, MBDBE rule (2019)
- Equipment must survive safe shutdown earthquake + multiple operating basis earthquakes
- NRC requires continuous monitoring of radioactive discharges and environmental samples

**Current detection:** Varies by facility — radiation monitors are primary, gas detection secondary.

**SFG advantage:** Meets seismic qualification more easily (no moving parts to shake loose). Solid-state inherently more survivable than FTIR in seismic events.

**Regulatory path:** Pitch to NRC as seismic-qualified gas monitor. IEEE 344 testing validates the claim.

## 6. Submarines / Naval Vessels

**Pain level: Moderate (captive market, long procurement cycles)**

- CAMS (Central Atmosphere Monitoring System) monitors O2, N2, CO, CO2, H2, H2O, refrigerants
- CAMS IIA (current gen): mass spectrometry + near-IR. Collins Aerospace (RTX subsidiary).
- Installed on all new US Navy nuclear subs, retrofitting existing fleet
- Deviations from atmosphere limits force: surface ventilation, mission abort, altered operations

**Current detection:** Mass spectrometry (CAMS IIA). Complex, expensive, maintenance-heavy.

**SFG advantage:** Simpler, cheaper, smaller than mass spec. Could supplement CAMS as redundant monitor.

**Note:** Active duty Navy perspective available but classified applications should not be pursued.

---

## 7. Disaster Response / Post-Earthquake

**Pain level: Moderate (episodic, high stakes when it happens)**

- After earthquakes at industrial facilities, need rapid gas monitoring to check for leaks
- FEMA deploys with generators and comm equipment, but gas monitoring is ad hoc
- Building assessment tools exist but gas monitoring is separate

**SFG advantage:** Device that was already monitoring before the earthquake keeps working after. Continuous monitoring through the event, not deployed after the fact.
